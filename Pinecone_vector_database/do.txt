Project Orion: Mars Habitat Communications Protocol
Document Version: 1.4
Last Updated: October 12, 2024

1. Project Overview
Project Orion is a joint initiative designed to establish a high-bandwidth, fault-tolerant communications network for the upcoming Ares V Mars surface habitat. The primary goal is to ensure zero-latency data transmission between the surface rovers, the orbital relay station, and Mission Control on Earth, accounting for solar interference and orbital alignments.

2. System Architecture
The communications array utilizes a tri-band relay system:
- Primary Link: Ka-band (26.5 to 40 GHz) for high-definition video and large telemetry data packets.
- Secondary Link: X-band (8.0 to 12.0 GHz) as a reliable backup for essential command and control protocols during atmospheric dust storms.
- Emergency Tether: Ultra-High Frequency (UHF) radio for proximity operations and suit-to-habitat voice communications.

3. Key Personnel
- Dr. Aris Thorne: Lead Communications Architect. Responsible for the overall network topology.
- Sarah Jenkins: Head of Orbital Mechanics. Manages the alignment schedules for the satellite constellation.
- Marcus Vance: Chief Security Officer. Oversees the AES-256 encryption protocols for all incoming command uplinks.

4. Deployment Timeline
- Phase 1 (January 2025): Launch of the first three orbital relay satellites via the heavy-lift vehicle.
- Phase 2 (June 2025): Initial ping testing and bandwidth stress-testing from lunar orbit.
- Phase 3 (November 2025): Final deployment of surface transceivers on the Martian surface.

5. Security Protocols
All data packets transmitted from Earth to Mars must undergo a dual-handshake verification process. If a packet is dropped due to solar radiation interference, the system will automatically hold the transmission in the orbital relay buffer for a maximum of 48 hours before requesting a resend from Earth.
